## How to read

Posts follow the same pattern across {{siteName}}:

- **Title** at the top, with author byline and publish date below it
- **Excerpt** below the byline — a 1-2 sentence summary of what the post covers
- **Body** in the main column, with H2 sections for major topics and H3 for sub-sections
- **Table of Contents** in the right column on desktop (only on posts long enough to warrant one — short posts skip it)
- **Tag chips** at the bottom of the body for cross-referencing
- **Author bio** below the tags
- **More articles** grid at the very bottom for discovery

If the body has FAQ items in its frontmatter (this one does), the questions get emitted as machine-readable structured data — search engines know the post answers specific questions and may surface them directly in results.

## How to follow new posts

Three options, in order of how often you check:

### Option A: RSS

The most reliable way. The RSS feed lives at [/blog/rss.xml](/blog/rss.xml). Paste it into any feed reader and new posts will appear there as they're published.

RSS readers are private (no account needed for {{siteName}}) and don't have an algorithm — you see every post, in order, exactly once. If you used to read blogs via Google Reader, RSS is what you want.

### Option B: Bookmark and check

Bookmark [/blog/](/blog/). Visit when you remember. Posts are listed newest-first.

This works fine for low-frequency reading.

### Option C: Sitemap monitoring

For automated systems (e.g. content aggregators): the [sitemap](/blog/sitemap-index.xml) gets updated on every post. Tools that watch sitemaps will detect new posts within minutes of publish.

Most humans should pick A or B.

## How to find specific topics

Each post is filed under one **category** and tagged with multiple **tags**.

- Click any tag chip at the bottom of a post → see everything tagged with that
- Click the category in the post metadata → see everything in that bucket
- Use the home page's main nav for top-level categories

Don't try to navigate by date alone — `/blog/page/2/` and onwards exists, but it's a slow way to find a specific post you remember reading. Tags + categories are faster.

## How to share

Every post has:

- A canonical URL (visible in the address bar; the same URL works for everyone)
- Open Graph metadata (rich previews on Twitter/LinkedIn/Slack/Discord/iMessage when you paste the URL)
- A Twitter Card (similar but Twitter-specific)
- An RSS-friendly title + excerpt for feed readers

Just paste the URL anywhere — the receiving platform will render it nicely.

## How content moves around

A behind-the-scenes note: posts are plain Markdown files (with some inline component support) in this site's git repository. The blog you see is built from those files. That has practical implications:

- Content survives this domain. If {{siteName}} ever moves to a different domain or platform, the posts are git-tracked text and can move with it.
- Edits and updates are visible in commit history.
- There's no database to back up; the source of truth IS the text files.

This is a deliberate architectural choice. Static blogs are massively faster, cheaper, and more durable than database-backed ones, at the cost of "hit publish and it's live in 2 seconds" being replaced by "hit commit and it's live in 30 seconds." For a content blog, that trade is fine.
